## Deep Thinking Patterns Observed

**1. Customer-framing present in analysis, absent in design—entrenched gap**
Evidence: Understanding track—billing communication insight, purchase-trigger + price-alert dual-branch synthesis. Solving track (all 6 modules)—no customer-harm framing in any design. The gap is consistent across the full curriculum.
What it reveals: Customer-outcome framing is an active skill when the exercise explicitly names stakeholders; it's not yet a default orientation in design work.

**2. "Beyond the obvious option" instinct—episodic but genuine**
Evidence: Decision tree module 2, scenario 2: *"Options: top 3 approaches beyond microservices."* Netflix archaeology: experimental infrastructure as product strategy signal. These extensions are unprompted.
What it reveals: The learner questions framing assumptions when given exploration freedom. This is a Think Big signal that is not yet installed as a default.

**3. Selective difficulty avoidance—first signal in the solving track**
Evidence: Lego challenges 2–3 (interface design, modular city—the exercises requiring specific contract specification) were not submitted. Part D (hashing bottleneck—explicit security/performance tradeoff) skipped. Module 4 (Lego) was the first module in the solving track requiring learner-designed outputs rather than analyses.
What it reveals: The learner's performance is strongest on analysis and identification exercises; exercises that require the learner to design and defend specific tradeoffs show lower engagement.

**4. Perfect structural pattern recognition—strongest in cohort**
Evidence: 12/12 question pattern identification. All multiple-choice exercises in understanding-problems: perfect. IOC module 1: chose correct answer (C, not D—rejected over-scoping). This is the cleanest record in the cohort.
What it reveals: The taught frameworks have been fully internalized as recognition structures. Transfer to generative use (designing a system from the frameworks) is the next development step.

**5. Correct-with-reasoning more developed than other learners**
Evidence: Problem-tree exercises explicitly state reasons for options: *"Q: Switch email provider vs add SMS backup for resets?"* At the leaf level, the decision and the alternatives are both named. Most learners name the decision; harsh names the alternatives.
What it reveals: Better-than-average awareness that a decision tree node has a structure: criteria → options → choice, not just choice.
