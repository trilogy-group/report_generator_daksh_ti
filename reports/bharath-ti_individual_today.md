## What to do next time

- **Do Module 1 now.** The intro is not a deliverable — the simulation is. `disagree-commit-trust-module1.md` is the next file. Complete it before anything else.
- **Before starting the simulation:** Write one real example from your career or this programme where you *should have* disagreed but didn't, or where you disagreed and then undermined the decision in execution. Label which phase the decision was in when you acted. This makes the subsequent simulation honest rather than theoretical.
- **In the simulation, take a hard position.** The PM is promising a broken dashboard to a customer in Module 1. The temptation is to soften your objection to avoid conflict. Don't. The rubric rewards specificity of counter-argument and clean execution post-decision, not politeness.
- **Practise the three-part structure out loud:** (1) Here is what I disagree with and why [with data]. (2) Here is the risk if we proceed [customer/business impact]. (3) Once you close the call, I will own my part fully — here is my specific commitment. If you can't produce all three parts, your backbone is incomplete.
- **Read the "commit" half as carefully as the "backbone" half.** Most engineers get the disagreement side right; they under-invest in the commitment side. Commitment means no backchannelling, no passive non-delivery, no "I told you so" if it fails.
